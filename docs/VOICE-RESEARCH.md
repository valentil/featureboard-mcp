# Voice Research: why AI text sounds like AI

Large language models produce text that is statistically and stylistically distinguishable from most human writing, even when it is fluent, correct, and individually unremarkable sentence by sentence. Two independent bodies of evidence point at this from different directions. Detection tools like GPTZero built classifiers around *perplexity* (how predictable each word is to a language model) and *burstiness* (how much sentence length and structure vary across a passage), because next-token-prediction training pushes models toward the statistically likely continuation and away from the length variance that comes from a human's shifting attention and memory. Separately, corpus linguists studying millions of PubMed abstracts, peer reviews, and news articles have found an abrupt, dateable spike in specific words — "delve," "tapestry," "underscore," "boasts," "realm" — right at the point ChatGPT-like tools became widely available, a pattern too sudden and too word-specific to be explained by topic drift or gradual style change.

Neither line of evidence amounts to a reliable way to *accuse* a specific document of being AI-written — perplexity/burstiness detectors have well-documented false-positive problems, and word-frequency shifts are a population-level signal, not a per-document proof. But together they describe, with real citations, a set of recognizable "tells": a narrow band of overused vocabulary, a handful of recurring sentence-level rhetorical moves (the contrastive pivot, the sycophantic opener, the tidy summary closer, the rule-of-three), and a flattened rhythm where sentences and list items all come out roughly the same shape. That's a useful target for a *self-editing* linter: something a writer runs against their own draft to catch habits that make otherwise-good writing read as generic.

This document catalogs those tells with their sources, then translates them into a rules catalog and a machine-readable JSON ruleset that `server/voice.js` (or an equivalent rule-based checker) can consume directly. It is deliberately scoped to editing assistance, not detection or accusation — see Limitations.

## Research summary

### Perplexity and burstiness (statistical detection)

- GPTZero's own explainer describes the two-signal approach directly: "Perplexity refers to the randomness of a text to a model... Higher scores indicate unpredictability, implying that a text is human-made," while "burstiness... measures variation in sentence length and structure," with AI text scoring low on both because "language models formulaically use the same rule to choose the next word... leading to low burstiness." — [What is perplexity & burstiness for AI detection? (GPTZero)](https://gptzero.me/news/perplexity-and-burstiness-what-is-it/)
- The broader mechanics of how classifiers combine these signals, and their limits, are summarized in [How Do AI Detectors Work? Techniques, Limitations & More (GPTZero)](https://gptzero.me/news/how-ai-detectors-work/).
- A stylometric comparison of AI vs. human sentence-length distributions found "AI sentences averaged 12.98 to 18.22 words while human sentences averaged 23.37 to 27.63 words," and that "LLMs exhibit similar distributions across different sentence lengths with less variation compared to human-generated sentences." — [Contrasting Linguistic Patterns in Human and LLM-Generated News Text (arXiv 2308.09067)](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2308.09067)
- A stylometric feature-based classifier ("StyloAI") built explicitly on sentence-length variance, punctuation patterns, and lexical-diversity features to separate AI and human text. — [StyloAI: Distinguishing AI-Generated Content with Stylometric Analysis (arXiv 2405.10129)](https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10129v1)
- A stylometric comparison of human vs. AI creative writing corpora, again using sentence-length and lexical-diversity metrics as primary discriminating features. — [Stylometric comparisons of human versus AI-generated creative writing (Nature Humanities & Social Sciences Communications)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-025-05986-3)

### Lexical overrepresentation (corpus / frequency studies)

- Kobak et al. analyzed over 15 million PubMed abstracts (2010-2024) and found an "abrupt increase in the frequency of certain style words" coinciding with ChatGPT's release, estimating "at least 13.5% of 2024 abstracts were processed with LLMs... reaching 40% for some subcorpora," an effect larger than the vocabulary shift caused by COVID-19. — [Delving into LLM-assisted writing in biomedical publications through excess vocabulary (Science Advances / arXiv 2406.07016)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adt3813) ([arXiv version](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.07016))
- Liang et al. tracked the surge of ChatGPT-characteristic adjectives in peer-review text submitted to AI conferences, using a maximum-likelihood mixture model to estimate that "6.5-16.9% of recent AI-conference review text was substantially LLM-modified, with activity peaking near submission deadlines." — [Monitoring AI-Modified Content at Scale: A Case Study on the Impact of ChatGPT on AI Conference Peer Reviews (arXiv 2403.07183)](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2403.07183)
- Juzek & Ward formally characterize "the puzzle of lexical overrepresentation," identifying 21 focal words (including "delve," "boast," "intricate," "tapestry") whose rise in frequency tracks LLM adoption, and test whether RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback) is a plausible cause — their model comparisons are "consistent with RLHF playing a role" in driving the overuse. — [Why Does ChatGPT "Delve" So Much? Exploring the Sources of Lexical Overrepresentation in Large Language Models (arXiv 2412.11385 / ACL Anthology COLING 2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.11385) ([ACL Anthology version](https://aclanthology.org/2025.coling-main.426/))
- A follow-up by the same authors extends the RLHF-causes-word-overuse hypothesis with additional alignment experiments. — [Word Overuse and Alignment in Large Language Models: The Influence of Learning from Human Feedback (arXiv 2508.01930)](https://arxiv.org/html/2508.01930v1)
- Concrete frequency numbers for individual words: "tapestry" appears in ChatGPT-generated text "at a rate of 102,959 words per billion" versus roughly 25x less often in the Project Gutenberg human-text corpus, and "testament" is "228 times more frequent in GPT texts compared to human-written texts." — [The Intricate Tapestry of ChatGPT Texts: Why LLM overuses some words at the expense of others?](https://textvisualization.app/blog/intricate-tapesty-of-chatgpt-texts/)
- A practitioner-facing summary aggregating overused abstract verbs ("delve, leverage, utilize, harness, streamline"), inflated adjectives ("pivotal, robust, innovative, seamless, cutting-edge"), and filler nouns ("landscape, realm, synergy, underpinnings"). — [Decoding AI Language: Common Words and Phrases in AI-Generated Content (Grammarly)](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/ai/common-ai-words/)

### Structural and rhetorical patterns (documented, mostly qualitative)

- The contrastive-pivot construction ("it's not X, it's Y," "not just X, but Y") is described as "one of the loudest giveaways of AI writing," a "sentence frame that organizes paragraphs before the writer has supplied much real substance," inflating a small point into apparent insight without adding content. — [Don't Write Like AI (1 of 101): "It's Not X, it's Y"](https://www.blakestockton.com/dont-write-like-ai-1-101-negation/)
- GPTZero's own writing-craft piece names the rule-of-three (tricolon) habit directly: "AI seems to be comforted by saying three things in a sentence," and warns that "a single tricolon is elegant; three back-to-back tricolons are a pattern recognition failure." — [How to Break Free from GPT's Rule of Three in Writing (GPTZero)](https://gptzero.me/news/the-rule-of-three/)
- A rhetorical-analysis essay ties the tricolon and antithesis habits together with em-dash overuse and bolded-list formatting as a recognizable composite style, calling the saturated result "slop." — [Why ChatGPT writes like that (Colin Gorrie, Dead Language Society)](https://www.deadlanguagesociety.com/p/rhetorical-analysis-ai)
- On em-dashes specifically: reporting found ChatGPT, DeepSeek, and Copilot "used em dashes heavily, well beyond what any human would likely do," while also documenting that not all AI output does this and that many humans use em-dashes as a genuine personal style, making the signal unreliable in isolation. — [Em Dashes, Hyphens and Spotting AI Writing (Plagiarism Today)](https://www.plagiarismtoday.com/2025/06/26/em-dashes-hyphens-and-spotting-ai-writing/), [No, an Em Dash Can't Help You Detect AI Text (How-To Geek)](https://www.howtogeek.com/no-an-em-dash-cant-help-you-detect-ai-text/)
- On sycophantic openers: OpenAI itself "conceded in April 2025 that GPT-4o had become 'overly flattering or agreeable — often described as sycophantic'" and rolled the behavior back, though residue phrases like "Great question!" and "You're absolutely right to push back on this" persist as a recognized tell. — [How to tone down the sycophancy in Claude, ChatGPT, and other AI chatbots (Transparency Coalition)](https://www.transparencycoalition.ai/news/how-to-tone-down-the-sycophancy-in-claude-chatgpt-and-other-ai-chatbots)
- Bolded-phrase-colon list formatting ("every bullet point... starts with a bolded phrase or sentence") is flagged as "extremely common in Claude and ChatGPT markdown output" and rare in genuine handwritten lists. — [Tropes - AI Writing Pattern Directory / AI Writing Tropes to Avoid](https://gist.github.com/ossa-ma/f3baa9d25154c33095e22272c631f5a1)
- Hedging and transition-word inflation ("it's important to note that," "moreover," "furthermore," "arguably") is documented as a recurring register shift in aggregated word-list studies. — [Decoding AI Language: Common Words and Phrases in AI-Generated Content (Grammarly)](https://www.grammarly.com/blog/ai/common-ai-words/)

Notes for the consuming linter:

- `pattern` strings are full JS regex literals including delimiters and flags (e.g. `"/\\bdelve\\b/i"`). Strip the leading/trailing `/` and the trailing flag substring to construct a `RegExp` object, or use a small helper that parses the literal form directly.
- `metric` rules do not carry a regex; the linter is expected to implement the four named metrics (`burstiness`, `tricolon-density`, `emdash-density`, `bold-colon-density`) as functions over the full document or a window, and compare against `threshold` per the direction described in each rule's `suggestion`.
- Severity is advisory (1-3); it does not gate whether a rule fires, only how it should be weighted or surfaced.

## Limitations

- **This is an editing aid, not a detector.** Every rule here is a heuristic tuned for false positives, not a forensic test. None of the underlying research (GPTZero's public methodology, the Kobak/Liang/Juzek-Ward corpus studies, or the stylometry papers) claims that matching these patterns proves a document was AI-written, and using it that way — to accuse a student, employee, or colleague — is a misuse of the tool and not something the evidence supports.
- **Humans use every one of these constructions too.** Em-dashes, the rule of three, "not X, but Y," even "delve" — all predate LLMs and remain part of normal human style for many writers. Flagging a passage means "this reads like the generic LLM register," not "a human did not write this."
- **Style varies by register.** Technical documentation, marketing copy, academic writing, and casual chat have different normal baselines for sentence-length variance, hedging, and transition-word density. A rule tuned against conversational prose will over-fire on formal writing and vice versa; thresholds in the JSON ruleset are starting points, not universals, and should be tunable per document type.
- **Word-frequency evidence is population-level.** The Kobak et al. and Juzek & Ward findings establish that specific words became more common in aggregate across millions of documents after LLM adoption — they do not and cannot establish that any single occurrence of "delve" in a specific document came from a model.
- **Detectors (and detector-like rules) degrade over time.** As noted in the stylometry literature, "these differences have diminished significantly" as models have improved and as writers consciously avoid known tells; a static rule list will need periodic revision as the target vocabulary and structures shift.
- **Rule-based matching has blind spots by construction.** A regex on "delve" catches the literal word, not a paraphrase; a burstiness threshold catches uniform rhythm, not uniform reasoning. A document can read as generic AI slop without tripping a single rule here, and can trip several rules while being entirely human-authored and fine.
- **Intended use:** run this against your own drafts to catch habits worth varying, not against someone else's writing to make a judgment about its authorship.
